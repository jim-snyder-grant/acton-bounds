# Monument Listings — Introduction

The next two sections cover the same 51 boundary monuments in two different ways: a map, and then one page per monument. They share a single numbering and coloring system, so it's worth reading this before either one.

## The overview map

The map that follows shows Acton's boundary as a thick gold line, running through the real roads and open water inside it. Each monument is marked with a hollow icon — a circle for a corner, a square for a street crossing, a triangle for a witness marker (see "Witness markers" below). Around the edge of the page are 51 small numbered, colored boxes, one per monument, connected to its icon by a line.

Those numbers and colors are the same ones used on every monument page that follows. Spot a monument on the map, and its number tells you which page to turn to. If you're reading digitally, clicking a box jumps straight there. The boxes are numbered the same way the monument pages are, but laid out around the edge of the page rather than tracing the boundary's own shape — match the numbers, not the layout.

## What's on every monument page

Each of the 51 monuments gets its own page, numbered 1–51, starting at the Acton/Concord/Maynard/Sudbury corner near the Senior Center and continuing around Acton's boundary. Every page has:

- A colored number and status at the top, so you can spot it at a glance.
- When it was visited, or searched for.
- Its coordinates, and where those coordinates came from.
- Nearby streets and landmarks.
- Notes on where to find it, and what it looks like.
- What the 1904 survey said about it, if anything.
- Photos, if we have them, in the order they were taken.
- Sometimes, a "Possible Next Steps" note — a tip for whoever picks this work up later, like which state agency to ask or where funding might come from. Most of these are on monuments that need follow-up, but a few Painted monuments have one too, if the monument itself needs repair.
- A small map, showing where the monument is. Click it to open the live version online.

## Reading the colored boxes

Each monument page — and each box on the overview map — carries a number (1 through 51) and a status, in the same two colors throughout the report:

- **Green — Painted.** Found, and marked with "2025."
- **Blue — Found.** Located, but not painted yet.
- **Orange — Couldn't paint.** Located, but it needs repair first.
- **Red — Not Found.** We looked, and didn't find it.
- **Gray — Documented.** It's in the old records, but nobody's been out to check yet.

## Corners and street crossings

Only 11 of the 51 monuments are true corners — spots where the town line bends, or a new town starts. Another 38 are places where an ordinary road crosses the boundary, and the last 2 are witness markers (see below). The name tells you which: a corner is named for the towns that meet there, like "Acton/Carlisle/Westford"; a street crossing adds the street, like "Acton/Concord Powder Mill Road"; and a witness marker says so in its own name. On the map, corners are circles, street crossings are squares, and witness markers are triangles.

## Where the coordinates come from

- **State** — official state survey data.
- **GIS iPad** — measured in the field with the Town's GPS-equipped iPad.
- **map estimate** — a best guess from a map or aerial photo, not a real measurement.

## Witness markers

A few monuments aren't at the true corner. They're "witness markers" — placed nearby because the real spot can't be marked (say, it's inside a house). On the map they're the triangle icons.

## Numbered pairs

A few corners share a name with a number after it, like "Acton/Carlisle 1" and "Acton/Carlisle 2." That's because the Acton/Carlisle line bends more than once, so there's more than one corner to name. The 1904 survey numbered them this way, and we kept the same numbers.

## Who's in the photos

"Witnesses" are the people who showed up to help clean and paint. Unless we note otherwise, they're Acton residents — someone from a neighboring town is marked with that town's name in parentheses.

## A few more details

Coordinates are rounded to about a meter of precision. Most photos link to a full-size version you can click through to. Monuments appear in counter-clockwise order around Acton, starting near the Senior Center at the Acton/Concord/Maynard/Sudbury corner.
