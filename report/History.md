# History of Acton's Bounds

Acton was incorporated in 1735, carved out of Concord's "New Grants" — land Concord had been given in 1665-7 — along with the separate "Wheeler farms." Its northern edge originally bordered Indian lands known as Nashoba, which later became the town of Littleton. In 1780, a slice of Acton's northern portion was annexed to Carlisle. Aside from that change, Acton's boundaries have stayed essentially the same as shown on official maps from 1794 and 1830 — nearly two centuries with almost no change to the line itself, even as the monuments marking it have needed regular attention.

## The 1904 Atlas itself

The volume this report leans on so heavily — *Boundary Lines of Acton, Bedford, Concord, Lincoln, Maynard, Sudbury, Wayland, Weston* (1904) — was one of 68 town-boundary atlases produced statewide between 1898 and 1915, first by Massachusetts's Topographical Survey Commission and then by its successor, the Board of Harbor and Land Commissioners. The fieldwork itself — triangulated surveys tying each town's boundary markers into higher-order state survey control, done jointly with the U.S. Coast and Geodetic Survey — was carried out in the 1890s; publication of the atlas volumes followed over the next two decades. Once complete, each volume was distributed free to every town, public library, and relevant state agency.

The Harbor and Land Commissioners were an unlikely home for this work — their day-to-day business was licensing wharves, dams, and dredging in tidewaters, not surveying inland town lines. They inherited the boundary project in 1901 when the Topographical Survey Commission that started it was dissolved, and carried it through to completion in 1914.

It's worth noting the survey's limits as well as its authority: MassGIS estimates that boundary marker coordinates from this era carry roughly ±3 feet of positional accuracy. A PDF of the entire 1904 atlas volume will be included in this project's DocuShare archive alongside the other supporting materials.

## Where each line came from

Acton shares a boundary with seven towns, and each line has its own origin story, set down in colonial and provincial law:

- **Concord** — the oldest of Acton's boundaries, part of the "Six Mile Square" Concord was laid out to in 1635, three miles in every direction from a center point near Nashawtuck Hill.
- **Carlisle** — defined in 1779-80, when Carlisle was incorporated as a district out of parts of Concord, Acton, Chelmsford, and Billerica.
- **Boxborough** — laid out as Acton's original westerly boundary when Acton itself was incorporated in 1735-36, and confirmed again in 1782 when Boxborough was carved out of Stow, Harvard, and Littleton.
- **Littleton** — the one line with no formal statute defining it at all. It's referred to in Concord's 1665 and 1667 land grants as the edge of the Nashoba lands, but nobody seems to have ever written down exactly where it ran; by the time Nashoba became Littleton in 1715, custom and long practice — confirmed by repeated perambulations and early maps — had settled the question.
- **Maynard** — the newest of Acton's neighbors, incorporated only in 1871 out of parts of Stow and Sudbury, at which point its entire boundary, including the stretch bordering Acton, was clearly spelled out for the first time.
- **Stow** — set in general terms back in 1735-36 when Acton itself was incorporated, as part of Stow's older northern line.
- **Westford** — part of a 9,800-acre tract granted to Concord in 1667, later described as the Concord line when Westford was incorporated in 1729-30.

Acton and Sudbury, notably, don't share a line at all — only a single point, the four-town corner where Acton, Concord, Maynard, and Sudbury all meet.

## A boundary dispute, settled by committee

Not every stretch of the line was uncontroversial. The Acton-Concord boundary was disputed enough that in 1747 the General Court appointed a committee to decide whether it ran in a straight line from a well-known old marker called "the Elm stump" to a certain white oak — or whether it should bend around roughly two acres of land and a dwelling instead. The committee found for the straight line, and their report was accepted and confirmed on December 11, 1747 — the same straight line Acton and Concord still share today.

## Why some roads carry different names today

Massachusetts had no requirement to name roads at all in 1904, which is why the 1904 survey behind this report describes monuments by informal directions instead — "on the road leading to Boxborough," or "about 680 feet southeast of the West Acton road." The first real push toward formal road names came with the state's Subdivision Control Law of 1953, which required new subdivision roads to be formally named as part of a town's approval process — though it didn't touch roads that already existed. Statewide 911 service, rolled out through the 1990s, extended formal naming to nearly every road still without one, since a 911 dispatch system depends on every address being unique.

Before either of these two legal changes, though, there's likely a more immediate driver: the rise of the automobile, and with it the demand for road maps and directions that meant something to a stranger rather than just a neighbor. That's a plausible thread, but not yet a proven one — it would take more digging (old road maps, Registry of Motor Vehicles records, or a conversation with people from the Acton Historical Society and elsewhere) to say anything more definite. Flagging it here as a research question rather than a settled fact.

Some roads, though, already had the names they carry today well before any of this. The 1904 survey itself refers to "Laws Brook road," "Strawberry hill road," and "Nagog hill road" — the same names still in use well over a century later. Others were called something different at the time: what's now Central Street was "the road between West Acton and the village of New Town"; what's now Pope Road was "the road leading to East Acton"; and what's now Main Street — on both the Carlisle and Maynard sides — was "the North Acton road" and "the South Acton road" respectively. Exactly when and why those particular roads were renamed is still an open question — one for future conversations with people from the Acton Historical Society and elsewhere.

Route *numbers*, at least, have a documented history distinct from road names. In April 1922, the six New England states agreed on a shared numbering scheme for the region's roads — through-routes got numbers 1–99, local roads 100 and up, with east-west routes generally taking odd numbers and north-south routes even — replacing an earlier, short-lived practice of painting colored bands on telegraph poles to indicate a route's direction. That system barely outlived the decade: when the nationwide U.S. Highway System arrived in 1926, many New England route numbers were reshuffled to avoid conflicts, and further renumbering continued into the early 1930s. Route 2, for instance, wasn't "Route 2" in 1922 at all — it was New England Interstate Route 7, and only took on its current single-digit identity once the 1926 changeover freed it up. So a road's number and a road's name usually arrived on two entirely different timelines — and neither one necessarily tells you when the road itself was first laid out.

## A road that disappeared: Parmenter Road

One thread worth following: the 1904 survey refers to "the Parmenter road" at two separate points along Acton's boundary — once on the Concord line, near what's now Old Stow Road, and again on the Maynard line, near what's now Parker Street. A short Parmenter Road still exists today, in Maynard, near the site of the old Parmenter farm. It's plausible that a single road once ran between these points — roughly northeast to southwest, from Maynard through what's now South Acton toward Concord — and simply disappeared from the map sometime in the last century, leaving only its name behind on one short remaining stretch.
