# Next Steps

This report closes out one round of perambulation, but it leaves behind a punch list — some short, concrete tasks, and a few longer-term questions worth handing to whoever picks this up next.

## Ready to finish

One monument, Acton/Concord Keefe Road, was found but not painted — the weather was simply too cold that day. It just needs a return visit whenever conditions allow; nothing else stands in the way.

## Monuments that may need repair

A few monuments were found but are in poor shape: one lying flat on the ground (Acton/Littleton 1), one leaning badly (Acton/Boxborough/Stow), and one partly buried (Acton/Maynard Parker Street). None of these are Acton's to fix alone — each sits on a shared line, so any repair would mean planning, funding, and doing the work together with the neighboring town.

## Monuments that may be documented elsewhere

Several monuments Acton couldn't find have a specific lead worth chasing: MassDOT may have records for the missing crossings at Acton/Boxborough Mass Ave (Rte 111), Acton/Boxborough/Littleton, Acton/Concord Rte 2, and Acton/Maynard Powder Mill Road (Rte 62). Two more have a private-party angle: the developers of the Avalon apartments (near the missing Acton/Westford corner) and of the Riviage development (near the missing Acton/Maynard Powder Mill Road crossing) may have pre-development site plans showing a monument that's since been buried or removed.

## Questions for neighboring towns

Concord found a monument at Powder Mill Road (Rte 62) in 2017 that Acton couldn't relocate — worth asking Concord for more detail. Maynard may have records on the missing Sudbury Road monument, lost during road improvements.

## Open historical threads

A few loose ends are more research questions than action items. The 1904 survey describes monuments on both the "easterly and westerly sides of the Cash Road" at what's now the Acton/Littleton Fort Pond Road crossing — but this report only accounts for one. What was found is most likely the westerly of the two; the easterly monument, which would have sat closer to the true boundary line, hasn't turned up. Other open threads: whether "Bear Hill Road" survives under a different name today (Acton/Westford Bear Hill Road), what corner the Fort Pond Road "W B" marker was placed to witness, whether Acton/Littleton Sarah Indian Way's monument survives under an older road alignment, and whether a monument exists at the edge of Ben's Woods near an unlocated 1904 corner (Acton/Carlisle unknown road). None of these block anything — they're simply interesting enough to leave documented for a future volunteer with more time to dig.

## Decisions Acton needs to make

At three crossings — Acton/Littleton Countryside Road, Acton/Littleton Lillian Road, and Acton/Concord Knox Trail — no monument was ever found, and it's plausible none was ever placed: these roads likely didn't exist yet when Acton stopped adding new monuments. Acton will need to decide whether any of these newer crossings warrant a monument going forward, or whether the absence is simply the historical record as it stands.

Two more crossings, Acton/Westford Bay Drive (East) and (West), raise a narrower question: both appear to be private roads, and state law's monument requirement may not apply to those at all — so even if Acton decides to add monuments at other missing crossings, these two might not need one regardless.

## Dating future work, and what comes next for the data

The Select Board has already discussed alternatives to painting a year onto each monument — a stick or a QR code, for instance — as a way to mark future perambulation dates without repainting. Separately, Acton's GIS Department plans to fold the coordinates and findings from this project into a new GIS layer, which should make future perambulations easier to plan and track. Between the two, the next crew to walk this line — in five years, or whenever Acton next takes it up — should have a considerably easier time of it than this one did.

## Who does the work next time

The neighboring Town with the most consistent record of doing perambulations in the 21st century is Sudbury. One thing they do differently than other Towns is that they vote every 5 years to give this work to Town staff, who have a very well worked-out process for doing the work. On the other hand, keeping this in the hands of the Select Board may make it easier to emphasize the community involvement aspects of the work.
