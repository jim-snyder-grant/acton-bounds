# Introduction

On October 21, 2024, Acton's Select Board voted 5-0 to direct two of its members, Jim Snyder-Grant and Dean Charter, to conduct a perambulation of the town's boundary — inspecting and, where needed, re-marking the monuments along the line between Acton and its seven neighboring towns. The vote grew out of an idea the two described at the outset as an opportunity for civic engagement, civic pride, sharing knowledge about the town, and the general benefits of being outside together.

The requirement that a Massachusetts town inspect and remark its boundary lines predates the United States by more than a century. In 1648, the General Court of the Massachusetts Bay Colony ordered:

> "...every towne shall set out their bounds within twelve months after their bounds are granted. And that when their bounds are once set out: once in the year three or more persons of a towne, appointed by the select men, shall appoynt with the adjacent townes to go the Perambulation bounds betwixt their said townes, and renew their marks; which marks shall be a great heap of stones, or a trench of six foot long and two foot broad... upon payne of five pounds for everie towne that shall neglect the same."
>
> — *1648, "Laws and Liberties of Massachusetts"*

Three and a half centuries later, a version of the same obligation survives as Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 42, Section 2:

> "The boundary markers of every town shall be located, the marks thereon renewed, and the year located marked upon the face thereof which bears the letter of the town locating its boundary, once every five years, by at least two of the selectmen of the town or by two substitutes designated by them in writing. The marking shall be made with a paint or other suitable marking material.
>
> The proceedings shall be recorded with the town clerk and the board of selectmen of the town in writing signed under penalty of perjury setting forth which boundary marks were located, and those which were not located. A copy of such records shall also be sent, by registered letter, to the town clerk and the board of selectmen of any contiguous town."

Two things changed along the way. The interval stretched from once a year to once every five years — the standard still in force today. And the penalty disappeared entirely: the section that once fined towns for neglecting the work, MGL Ch. 42 §3, was repealed in 1973 (Acts of 1973, c. 231, §2). For over fifty years, Massachusetts towns have marked their bounds, or not, with no legal consequence attached either way.

By the stone markings themselves, Acton's last substantial round of boundary work before this one was in 1986 — nearly forty years earlier. Of Acton's neighbors, Sudbury has been the most consistently active in recent decades: the Acton/Concord/Maynard/Sudbury corner shows a fresh mark roughly every five years going back to at least 1995.
