# The Work Behind This Report

## Locating the monuments

The work identified 51 monuments: the corners and street crossings listed in Acton's 1904 boundary survey, plus a handful of additional points where a public road crosses the town line. Jim and Dean located and assessed each one, and, where it could be found, painted it with the year to continue a marking tradition that goes back generations (older monuments often carry a stack of past painted years). Most monuments were found and painted; a smaller number were located but needed repair before painting, and a few couldn't be found at all despite a search.

Where a neighboring town had its own recent perambulation report on file, Acton compared notes: Boxborough (2007), Concord (2017), Stow (2007), and Sudbury. The comparison surfaced a few genuine discrepancies worth flagging to those towns — two monuments Concord's 2017 crew couldn't locate, for instance, were found and painted by Acton in 2025 — and confirmed agreement on most others. Carlisle, Littleton, Westford, and Maynard's reports weren't available for comparison; Acton requested them once, without a response.

## Reaching out for additional witnesses

Early in the project, Jim and Dean emailed the Select Boards (and in some cases Town Managers) of each neighboring town, explaining the plan to inspect and paint the shared boundary monuments and inviting them to take part. Some towns responded. Because painting depends on dry weather, the specific monuments to visit on any given outing were usually decided only a day or two ahead — and anyone who'd shown interest in the first round of emails was recontacted before each outing to see if they wanted to join.

Representatives from Littleton, Sudbury, Stow, and Boxborough each joined at least one outing; Concord, Maynard, and Westford were invited but nobody from those towns ended up coming along. Within Acton, word of the project also spread informally, and a few residents asked to be included on future visits and joined in as well.

Town staff weren't part of the fieldwork, but became essential once it came time to build this report. Acton's Planning Department helped track down information on a few monuments that couldn't be found. The GIS Department trained Jim to use a GPS-equipped iPad connected to the town's ArcGIS server, which produced much more accurate coordinates than earlier estimates — and the department plans to fold this project's findings into a new GIS layer of its own.

A typical outing covered a handful of monuments and ran up to about three hours — though sessions often wrapped up sooner, either because the group finished early or because people were ready to head home. At each monument, the marker was cleaned up and, if conditions allowed, painted with the year using Satin Black Protective Enamel Rust-Oleum paint; painting requires a dry surface and warm-enough weather, so some visits were for locating and assessing a monument only, with painting to follow on a later outing. Like any outdoor fieldwork in New England, the usual hazards — poison ivy, ticks, uneven terrain — came with the territory.

## A note on Claude's role

This report was produced with the help of Claude, Anthropic's AI assistant, working in two complementary roles: one instance (Claude Code) handled the technical pipeline — reading the monument data, generating each monument's page, building the overview map, and assembling the final document — while another (claude.ai) handled writing and analysis, including this section. The two coordinated through the project's GitHub repository. A report of this depth and consistency — 51 monuments, cross-referenced against four neighboring towns' records, each with its own page, map, and photo — would have been a much heavier lift for a two-person volunteer effort just a few years ago.

## Where this work is archived

The finished report, along with the underlying photos, is archived on Acton's DocuShare system — the town's permanent public record. It can be found under Boards & Committees / Select Board / Perambulation, or [https://doc.actonma.gov/dsweb/View/Collection-19415](https://doc.actonma.gov/dsweb/View/Collection-19415). The project's code and documentation are also published in a public GitHub repository at [github.com/jim-snyder-grant/acton-bounds](https://github.com/jim-snyder-grant/acton-bounds), so another Massachusetts town — or Acton itself, next time — can reuse the process. Google Drive was used as a working folder during the project but isn't a durable archive; it won't be maintained once the report is complete.
